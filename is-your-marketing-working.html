How to actually tell if your marketing is working.

Most business owners genuinely can't tell if their marketing is paying off. The dashboards are full of numbers that don't mean anything and quiet about the ones that do. Here's how to cut through it, without hiring a data team.

IGNORE VANITY METRICS FOLLOW THE MONEY FEW NUMBERS, READ OFTEN

The reason most people can't tell if their marketing works is that they're watching the wrong numbers. Likes and followers and impressions go up, so it feels like something's happening, but none of those numbers have to turn into money, so they don't tell you anything about whether it's working.

Working means one thing: are you making more than you're spending, and is that gap growing. Everything else is a clue at best and a distraction at worst. So before you look at a single dashboard, throw out the numbers that only measure attention, and keep the few that measure money.

The vanity numbers to stop staring at

Followers. Likes. Impressions. Reach. Open rates in isolation. Traffic, on its own. None of these are worthless, but none of them tell you if your marketing is working, because a business can have all of them going up and still be losing money. They measure whether people noticed you. They don't measure whether that turned into anything. If a number can go up while your bank account goes down, it's not the number to judge your marketing by.

The numbers that actually tell you

You only need a handful, and you don't need special tools to get most of them.

What it costs to get a customer. Take what you spent on marketing in a period and divide it by the number of customers you got. That's your cost to acquire one customer. If it's climbing, something's getting less efficient. If it's falling, something's working. This one number cuts through more noise than any dashboard.

What a customer is worth to you. How much does the average customer spend with you, in total, over their whole relationship, not just the first order. Put this next to the cost of acquiring them and you have the entire game in two numbers: it costs you X to get a customer worth Y. If Y is comfortably bigger than X, your marketing works. If it isn't, no amount of likes will save it.

Conversion rate. Of the people who land on your site or see your offer, what percentage actually buy. This tells you whether the problem is getting attention or converting it, which are two completely different fixes. Rising conversion means your message and offer are landing.

Revenue you can trace back. Not total revenue, the revenue you can actually connect to a marketing effort. "We ran this and sales from it went up" is worth more than any engagement stat. When you can draw a line from a specific thing you did to money that came in, that's the line that tells you it worked.

The whole test in one line

It costs you X to get a customer worth Y. If Y is bigger than X and the gap is growing, your marketing is working. If you don't know your X and your Y, that's the first thing to fix, before anything else.

How to read them without drowning

You don't need a data team or a wall of dashboards. Pick three or four of the numbers above, write them down once a month, and watch the direction they move. Direction over time beats precision. You're not trying to measure to the decimal, you're trying to answer one question: is the gap between what a customer costs and what a customer is worth getting better or worse. Everything you do in marketing should be aimed at widening that gap, and a simple monthly check tells you whether it's happening.

If you've been judging your marketing by how it feels or how busy it looks, switching to these few numbers is often a shock. Things you were proud of turn out to do nothing, and quiet things turn out to carry the business. That clarity is the point. You can't fix what you can't measure, and you can't measure it with the wrong numbers.
